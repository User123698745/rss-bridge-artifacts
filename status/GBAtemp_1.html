    • Review Anbernic RG406V

    2024-10-18 09:48:32

    by: KiiWii

    The Anbernic array of devices grows with every passing month, and almost one year ago, I posted a review for the Anbernic RG405V on GBAtemp, singing the praises of the then-current handheld. However, I wasn't entirely convinced of the overall emulation capability when it came to the playability of PS2 or GC games on the system.

    The Anbernic RG406V sounds as incremental as you can get, though it does cram in many updated components to reinforce the fact that the RG405V is now antiquated:

    Technical information:

    • Colours available: Black Transparent / Beige White / Gray
    • Screen: 4-inch IPS screen, OCA full lamination, multi-touch, 960*720 resolution
    • CPU: Unisoc T820 processor, 6nm EUV 5G SOC chip octa-core 1*[email protected] + 3*[email protected] + 4*[email protected]
    • GPU: Quad-core Mali-G57, 850MHz
    • RAM: 8GB LPDDR4X
    • Storage: 128G UFS2.2
    • Expandable Memory: Supports up to 2TB Micro SD
    • System: Android 13
    • WIFI/Bluetooth: 2.4/5G WIFI 802.11a/b/g/n/ac,Bluetooth 5.0
    • Games Support: Android games & emulators
    • Speaker: High-fidelity dual speakers
    • Battery: Polymer lithium battery 5500mAh, battery life 8 hours
    • Charging: Support 5V/2A charging, approximately 3.5 hours to fully charge
    • RGB lighting effect: 16 million colour RGB lighting, with built-in modes including constant light, breath, marquee, chasing, rainbow, gaming, support customise the colour and adjust the brightness.
    • Joystick: Large-angle 3D Hall joystick
    • Other functions: six-axis gyroscope sensor, high-speed fan + heat pipe active heat dissipation, support FOTA wireless upgrade, online multi-player, streaming, wireless projection screen, USB Type-C supports 1080p Display Port output, vibration motor,
    • 3.5mm stereo earphones, energy-saving settings, joystick light control, display brightness, etc.
    • Accessories: USB charging cable, colour box, user manual, screen protector
    • Console specification: length 14.5cm; width 10.5cm; height 2.9cm; weight 0.289 kg

    Beefed-up Specs = Better Performance

    To briefly summarise the enhancements in this handheld, we can see that Anbernic has increased the screen resolution from 640 x 480 to 960 x 720, doubled the RAM from 4 GB to 8 GB, upgraded the GPU from a G52 to G57 Mali, and upgraded the CPU from a T618 to a T820 with all cores clocking higher than the previous model. The one main downside of this increased power consumption is the decrease in battery life. The battery remains the same Polymer lithium battery 5500mAh unit, though on this version it is rated for just 8 hours of playtime, whereas the RG405V was rated for 9 hours.

    Incidentally, the RG406V I was sent did not come "fully loaded", so I took the SD card from the RG405V and plugged it into the RG406V to do a more thorough, apples-to-apples, comparison. This was great because I could test the two virtually side by side and get a grasp on what had improved if anything!

    The first thing I wanted to check was how the higher-end emulators benefitted from this upgraded loadout, and I have to say that PS2 and PSP have seen a decent uptick in frame consistency across even the most taxing games.

    AetherSX running God Of War II ran phenomenally out of the box. With my previous review alluding to PS2 being a bit of a stretch for the handheld, this is not the case with the RG406V which runs PS2 games brilliantly. I will immediately temper expectations a little here though because nothing is perfect, or as flawless as the OG hardware, and in places, there were frame drops and small sections of stutters. By and large, this handheld kept the game chugging along enough for me to say that it is fully playable with only a few minor slowdowns in places.

    PSP sees a nice uptick too with Tekken 5 and 6 running flawlessly in my testing. I definitely think that Anbernic listened to my critique regarding out-the-box configurations because this set-up flies just fine!

    Gamecube also benefits a little from this hardware, with Wind Waker running beautifully at a stable 28-30 FPS on MMJR2, though once again F-Zero AX failed to improve beyond the stuttery 23 FPS slideshow I remember from the RG405V.

    N64 was again a solid outing. Conkers Bad Fur Day and Goldeneye X ran perfectly, with little issue other than the same GFX glitches here and there that the previous handheld saw too.

    Most of what happens on these devices comes down to the emulator involved, and most of the emulators have inherent issues with certain games. I have to say that PS2 and 3DS emulation have both improved on the RG406V though the 3DS emulation, in particular, is still a little hit-and-miss. Fluidity Spin Cycle ran perfectly this time (no stuttering) but A Link Between Worlds, though it ran better, had weird missing GFX in places. This was a similar story for Super Mario 3D World where it looked like Mario had been dunked in tar, and the framerate was questionable.

    It's worth remembering that Citra recently got the Nintendo treatment, so other forks such as Lime3DS and Mandarine exist too, but both share the trait of some games working fine, whereas others stutter or have slight GFX issues.

    Identical Form Factor & Speakers, Better Screen

    The RG406V is pretty much identical in size and weight to the RG405V, and the comfortability of the device is equally as good. I didn't find myself cramping up or getting wrist fatigue holding the device. The Hall Effect sticks and the D-Par are both virtually perfect on this handheld, and every game I played with them felt great, though some dead zones needed tweaking in N64 games, to get the more authentic OEM N64 feel, for example.

    Still, I'm not too fond of the sound quality of the built-in speakers. They're too tinny and flat sounding to offer a pleasing audio experience with these games. Thankfully I could pair my Bluetooth headset to the RG406V, or use the 3.5mm headphone jack, to use my headphones or connect it to a speaker or soundbar if you wanted to.

    The screen on the RG406V is a triumph. Colours pop, there's no ghosting, and the viewable angle is pretty sweet. Overall, the resolution looks far superior to the RG405V, with it outputting to a 4:3 720p screen. Oddly I noticed how washed out and dull the colours looked while casting. Though casting is a nice touch, it isn't bulletproof when it comes to displaying colour and vibrance. Playing Crazy Taxi 2 and Lylat Wars on my TV wirelessly with the handheld acting as a controller was superbly fun though, imagine taking it on holiday with you!

    Bearing in mind that I tested all my games at native resolution, I had virtually no issue with GBA, DC, PSP, 3DS, GC, PS2, and Wii games (emulator permitting) and they all looked fantastic on the portable. If you have issues with a certain game, install an updated emulator or a mod for the game, and nine times out of ten: you have a solution to play the games you want to play.

    The RG406V is definitely a worthy upgrade to the RG405V, almost all issues I had with the previous model have been addressed and the overall experience is fantastic throughout. Whether it's retro gaming or modern gaming via Android 13 and the Google Play Store, there is always something to play on this device, with no need for a PC in the middle to have to obtain content.

    Verdict

    What We Liked ...
    • Beefier specs all around
    • Higher resolution screen
    • Less glare and better colour saturation
    • Improved joystick feel
    What We Didn't Like ...
    • Same speaker woes
    • Less battery life
    8.5
    out of 10

    Overall

    The RG406V ups the ante from the RG405V and really makes a difference in the emulation stakes, with emulators able to harness the extra grunt under the hood if they are able to. From NES to NDS, DC, GC, Wii 3DS & PS2; the RG406V is a solid device for 4:3 ratio emulation on the go as well as Android 13-based gaming!

Today, after almost an exact year since the original teaser, Analogue has finally unveiled the console, as well as the start date for the preorders and, most importantly, its price tag.

    It's important to note that this isn't software emulation, as most other devices that offer N64 compatibility might have, as this is an FPGA (Field Programmable Gate Array) device, meaning that the original hardware of the console is being recreated in-hardware as well thanks to the different hardware configurations that FPGAs offer. In other words, hardware emulation.     This new FPGA-based N64 console by Analogue will begin its pre-orders on October 21st, 2024 at 8am PDT, going for a price of $250 USD. Some of the features this console will have are:
    • 100% compatible with every original N64 game ever made.
    • Region Free.
    • Bluetooth LE.
    • Dualband Wifi.
    • Four original-style controller ports.
    • Entirely new, next generation Analogue hardware featuring 3DOS.
    • Engineered entirely in FPGA. No Emulation.

    ​


    Additionally, Analogue worked closely with 8BitDo to create a brand new N64 modern controller with Bluetooth connectivity and a modern form factor, alongside many of the other quality of life and other features that 8BitDo controllers are known for, like Hall effect sticks.

    • Review Europa

    2024-10-12 20:30:46

    by: Prans

    Europa’s story begins when Zee, an android resembling a young boy, receives a message from his “father”, Adam, which encourages him to venture forth across Europa, the moon he is currently in. The latter has been terraformed by humans but they seem to be strangely missing. 

    As Zee explores this moon of Jupiter, he encounters diary entries from Adam which slowly reveal the history of Europa’s terraforming efforts and the fate of its human settlers. These recordings, scattered across the ruins of utopia, depict a relatable narrative of humanity and nature.

    A short journey across a Ghibli-like world

    The small development team at Novadust Entertainment have been open about their Ghibli inspirations for Europa and it shows. Its vibrant and colourful landscapes filled with ruins, towering peaks and curious creatures feel like an interactive Studio Ghibli animation.

    Another apparent influence is that of The Legend of Zelda with its focus on exploration, but Europa adopts a more relaxed pace. It encourages players to take their time to take in the gorgeous environment, explore their surroundings and fill out their bestiary with sketches of the peculiar “gardener species” left behind to cultivate Europa. 

       

    Despite its Ghibli and Zelda inspirations, Europa’s designs are original and feel different than the latter examples. It really thrives on moments of quietude, rather than conflict, and focuses on the simple joy of discovery accompanied by soothing tunes.

    Stress-free exploration across Europa

    Assisting Zee’s explorations is the Zephyr jetpack. It allows Zee to glide across valleys, boost jump to platforms and stomp on weak floors. It’s really the game’s main mechanic and can even be considered as an extension of Zee’s form. This means of traversal and exploration is original and provides a relaxing experience when Zee chains boots to glide across large distances akin to a bird riding the wind. This sense of freedom pairs well with Europa’s open-world design and cosy aspect.

     

    While it’s a mostly stress-free experience, there are some environmental hazards to bear in mind. Zee will come across turrets and mines that are better avoided. Getting hit by them won’t result in a game-over screen, but will see Zee momentarily stunned. Such encounters serve as a gentle nudge to try a different approach and also make the experience less of a walking simulator. Furthermore, there are some light puzzle elements to keep the player engaged. They aren’t particularly difficult and the game as a whole is rather forgiving.

     

    That said, the solo exploration of Europa’s ruins has some downsides. Across its 3-4 hour playtime, Zee does not interact with any NPC and it’s only Adam’s notes that serve as a storytelling mechanic. The experience is a lonely one across an empty and desolate planet which would have benefited from more interaction and more exposition of its lore as Europa seems to have more to tell than what Adam lets on.

    Europa: a tranquil adventure

    Europa is available on Switch and PC; and it feels right at home on a handheld. Having played the Switch version, it is a decent version, although not the best one. There are occasional dips in frame rate and noticeable pop-ins, particularly in busier areas. I also experienced crackling audio during some cutscenes and chapter transitions. Even if these issues don’t detract too much from the overall experience, a bit more polish could elevate Europa’s presentation on Nintendo’s handheld.

    Overall, Europa is an original, tranquil experience. It wants you to lose yourself in its beautiful, desolate world. It does achieve this aim with its focus on stress-free exploration but the sense of solitude does not always play in its favour. That said, it is more than a decent debut for the indie team and I hope they revisit this universe again.
