“Wake Up, Maggie, I Think I Got Somethin’ To Say To You”

Apartment Complex, Babysitting, Bad Behavior, Friends, Lazy/Unhelpful, Liars/Scammers, Non-Dialogue, Roommates, USA | Friendly | October 21, 2024


My husband and I had a close friend in college; we’ll call her Maggie. The three of us practically lived together between our dorm rooms, and things were really great. Fast forward a few years; we stayed close even as we all moved around the country. She even flew across the country to be in our wedding.

So, it was only natural that when Maggie kept telling us about roommate troubles in her town and wanting to start new somewhere, we offered for her to move in with us. We knew she didn’t have a solid income or many marketable skills for more than minimum wage, so we made a deal. She would watch our soon-to-be-born son as a live-in nanny only while both my husband and I worked (no overtime, weekends, nights, etc., starting by 8:50 and done before 4:00), get paid $500 per month, and have zero bills. We asked that she do her fair share of chores and give us a month of notice if the baby was getting to be too much since she wasn’t sure how much she liked caring for babies.

All went well at first. I taught Maggie everything our son needed as an infant and helped her figure out a schedule so she could still have me-time when he slept. Then, she stopped doing her share of the chores. At first, she would “do” the chores but half-a** them — think putting away dishes with crusty food still caked on, pushing dirt around without sweeping it up, “not seeing the problem” with burnt food caked on the stove, things like that.

I expressed discomfort at this, and my husband immediately jumped on Maggie’s side to say she was overworked from the baby. I was able to rearrange my work schedule to be home more and help more, but then Maggie stopped doing chores at all unless my husband was there to see her try to do them. Saying something didn’t help, so I didn’t say anything. (I know, I should have spoken up again. I didn’t want to ruin a long-standing friendship.)

After this, Maggie started sending me messages all day if I wasn’t home to tell me her every move. That wasn’t horrible on its own, but she’d tell me every step she took only for me to come home and find out half of what she said she did hadn’t been done — like her saying she had washed the baby clothes because he threw up, and I’d find out she either ran a whole wash for one outfit or none of it had actually been done. At that point, I brought up again that I thought she wasn’t holding up our agreement. She became depressed and wouldn’t talk to me for a few days, and my husband told me my standards of clean were too high. I pointed out that the baby was crawling around on dirt, dust, and old dried food that was never being swept up unless I had time to do it. Both he and (eventually) Maggie said my standards of clean were outrageous.

I started to believe them and tried to take over all the cleaning, which was a struggle since my husband hadn’t ever really been around babies so he needed a lot of help learning to care for our son, he was starting to not complete chores he used to before Maggie moved in as he believed my standards were just becoming too high so he didn’t actually need to do that much work to clean the house, and my husband and I both worked physically and mentally demanding jobs. (He teaches middle-schoolers and I’m a foster care child therapist.)

After that, Maggie went away for a week. When she came back, she told my husband and me that she was done watching our son and wanted to find a new job. We asked her if she meant immediately or if we could have the month she promised us to find alternate child care, and she said it was effective immediately because she “just isn’t cut out to work with babies and only realized it on her week away”. We changed the agreement to be that she would pay a little less than a third of all household bills (working out to about $500 as we live in an expensive area).

This all came to a head when I caught Maggie yelling and screaming at my cat to shut up because she had a headache. This made my son wake up from his nap and start to cry, but she did not seem to notice while she continued to yell at my cat. I stepped in, told her that was completely inappropriate, and took my son and cat to hide in the baby room until my husband came home. I told him in tears what had happened, and he confronted Maggie. Maggie said she scolded my cat for being bad but denied the rest.

After a long talk, my husband agreed that it was time for her to find her own apartment. Maggie was strangely calm about this and insisted that she wanted to keep a good relationship with us. I tried to help her find a place to stay, sending every apartment opening I could find in her price range that wasn’t in a terrible neighborhood so she could still be safe. She kept saying she applied for them when I sent them, but her behavior didn’t improve. I saw my cat and baby both start to get highly anxious and fearful when navigating our apartment. My cat even started to lose weight dramatically from the stress.

After eight months of Maggie trying to find an apartment and failing, my husband and I decided to see if there were any apartments or not horribly priced houses we could afford. We found a house that I didn’t hate and he enjoyed, but it was the top of our budget and the cheapest thing we could find in our area.

We told Maggie about it and that we were moving out, breaking our lease to do so. Maggie’s only response was to smile and say, “Oh, cool, so my girlfriend can move in here like she and I talked about.” Mind you, I still feel that that apartment was our family’s home and we were being forced out by someone I viewed as a dear friend.

Since moving out, our son has thrived and isn’t anxious about adults anymore. Our cat has also gotten back to a healthy weight and is rambunctious again. Individually, none of the things Maggie did was a big deal. I also recognize that I could have handled a lot of it differently, and I recognize that. It doesn’t change the way she played my husband against me and twisted things to make herself the victim. She currently has both of us fully blocked, so no happy resolution as to whether she’s still in that apartment with her girlfriend. The last we saw or heard of her was when she was trying to move her girlfriend into the apartment the same day we had told her for weeks that we were moving our things out.

The Closer To The Top They Get The Harder The Fall

Bosses/Owners/Managers, Call Center, England, Government, UK | Right | October 21, 2024


I work in a government call centre, not too far off from a governmental advice bureau. I get a call near the end of the day on a Friday.

Caller: “I would like to speak to the person at the top, please.”

Me: “If you could tell me what you’re calling about I can direct your call better.”

Caller: “You can direct my call to the person in the department with the highest authority.”

Me: “I can direct your call, sir, but first I would need to know what it’s regarding.”

Caller: *Repeating himself, not raising his voice.* “You can direct my call to the person in the department with the highest authority.” 

I put him on hold and tell my manager about the call. He says to transfer it and he takes over.

Manager: “Hello, sir, I am a manager here. How can I assist you?”

Caller: “Are you the manager at the top of your department?”

Manager: “I am qualified to handle all calls that come into our department.”

Caller: “That is not what I asked. Are you the manager at the top of your department, or do you report to someone higher?”

Manager: “I do report to someone higher, but as I was saying, I am qualified to handle all calls that come into our department.”

Caller: “No thank you. You can direct my call to the person in the department with the highest authority.”

After a couple more rounds of this, my manager gives up and transfers him to the tier 3 manager. This is the highest a caller can go.

T3 Manager: “Hello, sir, I am the top-level manager here. How can I assist you?”

Caller: “Who do you report to?”

T3 Manager: “My manager is not relevant to the resolution of any type of call we handle, sir. I’m as high up the totem pole, you’re going to get.”

Caller: “No thank you. You can direct my call to the person in the department with the highest authority.”

T3 Manager: “For callers, I am the person in the department with the highest authority.”

Caller: “No thank you. I want to talk to the person above you.”

T3 Manager: “I will consider your request, but only if you tell me the nature of your inquiry. I will not entertain this conversation any further unless you go into some detail.”

Caller: “No thank you. I want to talk to the person above you.”

The T3 manager mutes the call and looks at all of us.

T3 Manager: “Is this guy a bot? Or a parrot?”

We all honestly did not know.

T3 Manager: “Fine, two can play at this game.”

He unmutes the call.

T3 Manager: “I will consider your request, but only if you tell me the nature of your inquiry. I will not entertain this conversation any further unless you go into some detail.”

Caller: “No thank you. I want to talk to the person above you.”

T3 Manager: “I will consider your request, but only if you tell me the nature of your inquiry. I will not entertain this conversation any further unless you go into some detail.”

Caller: “Are you just repeating yourself?”

We snapped the circuit!

T3 Manager: “I will repeat the same information back at you unless you give me more to go on.”

Caller: “I don’t need to give you more to go on. I would like to talk to the person at the very top, please.”

T3 Manager: “Why?”

Caller: “I would like to explain it to them.”

The T3 Manager mutes him again and starts a conversation with someone else on another line. As a government department, we are technically allowed to hang up on problem callers, but we really try not to. The T3 Manager talks to someone and asks if they’d be willing to help.

About three minutes later Miss Bigwig herself walks onto the call center floor. I see her in the building occasionally, but she’s a big deal and technically manages the whole call center floor I work on, not to mention multiple other departments.

She briskly walks over to the T3 Manager.

Miss Bigwig: “He still on the line?”

T3 Manager nods.

Miss Bigwig: “Okay, pass me the headset.”

She takes it over, and we all continue to listen in.

Miss Bigwig: “This is [Miss Bigwig] and I hear you wanted to talk to me.”

Caller: “Are you the person at the top?”

Miss Bigwig: “Yes.”

Caller: “Who do you report to?”

Miss Bigwig: “The Prime Minister.”

The caller goes silent for a moment, except for a subtle “hmm” as he probably considers if this is high enough for him to finally say what he wants to say.

Caller: “Then I guess you will do. I am calling about my wife’s visa case file. I need it to be completed this weekend so we can fly out for a wedding.”

Miss Bigwig: “You’re calling up to expedite a visa application?”

Caller: “Yes.”

Miss Bigwig: “Congratulations, you called the wrong number. The correct number for that is [number similar to ours], and that line closed at 5 pm, which was ten minutes ago. If you’d told the agent you first got on the line your request they could have even transferred you, but that will be impossible now. They open again at 9 AM on Monday. Have a great weekend.”

And with that Miss Bigwig ended the call before the caller could respond. We are all looking at her.

Miss Bigwig: “What are you all doing? Go home! Have a nice weekend!”

Awww, Do The Poor Widdle Empwoyers Have To Fowwow The Wawww?

Bosses/Owners/Managers, Canada, Criminal & Illegal, Employees, Jerk, Karma/Comeuppance, Money, Non-Dialogue, Office, Ontario, Petty Revenge, Tech Support | Working | October 21, 2024


This story is about a good friend’s brother. Let’s call him “Bob”. Bob has been fiddling with computers since he was a kid and knows them pretty well. As with most IT people, he’s moved from job to job. The employer he worked for was a service/distribution company, and there were two IT employees. The company was located in Ontario, Canada.

About three years ago, Bob’s employer decided to modernize their software. They had separate programs for Dispatching, for Inventory, and for Payroll and Finances, and it was complicated moving information from one program to the other. They decided to get an ERP (Enterprise Resource Planning) program, and Bob recommended one that he knew inside out from a previous employer.

For those of you who don’t know, an ERP program handles everything: purchase orders, sales, inventory, personnel, vendors, customers, all of it. You can run a report and find out which customer has bought the most of a particular part in the last year, which salesman has improved his numbers the most, which vendor has the fastest delivery time, which shipper packed the most orders, etc.

Everyone in the company used the ERP program, but it was very complicated, and they used the aspects of it that related to their position. For example, the Receiver would accept a shipment, verify the quantity, and confirm it was received… and the inventory stats would be available to the Sales people if they wanted to look up how many were on hand. The Receiver didn’t care what the price was, or who the vendor was; he just did his job.

Bob was run ragged during the implementation process, but he managed to train most of the employees on their aspects, and after a few months, everything was running fairly smoothly. Bob still got tickets for tweaks in the operation of the software and occasional hardware IT issues.

Then, the company decided to expand their footprint and started marketing into different time zones.

That messed things up. Atlantic Canada is ninety minutes behind, so if someone sent an email or an order at 8:00 am their time, it would arrive at 6:30 am Ontario time. Pacific Canada is three hours ahead, so an email sent at 3:00 pm Vancouver time would arrive at 6:00 pm in Ontario. This stretched out the day, so many staff came in early and worked late.

Bob would arrive at 8:00 am. Some people demanded his immediate assistance and were annoyed that he didn’t respond instantly, even though their requests were submitted before his start time. Same with late in the day; his phone would ring at dinnertime with people who wanted help right now.

Management decided to stagger Bob’s and his IT colleague’s shift times. Bob would start at 6:00 am and work until 2:30 pm, and his colleague would start at 10:30 am and work until 7:00 pm. Bob’s colleague had kids and refused the shift change. The employer insisted. The colleague quit.

That meant that Bob was the only person in the IT department. The employer said they would look to hire a new IT guy, but they had trouble finding one who knew the ERP system — and they were offering well under a market value salary.

Bob asked for a raise and was denied. Then, he wanted overtime, and the employer told him that as an IT specialist, he was exempt from overtime laws in Ontario. Bob looked it up, and the employer was correct. This went on for some time. Bob knew lots of IT people socially. They told him what the company was offering, and Bob knew that they wouldn’t find another tech.

Things went downhill from there. Bob would get chewed out if he missed a call or an email, no matter what time it came in. He had to train new hires in the ERP system, as well as take care of the hardware. He asked repeatedly for better compensation, and he was denied… so he planned to get a new job.

Everyone in the company was on salary, and many of them came in early and stayed late. Ontario labour law stated that even salaried workers were entitled to overtime after forty-four hours a week, unless they were Managers or Supervisors.

Bob had access to the entirety of the ERP program. When a user signed in, the time was logged, and even if they didn’t sign out, it would log them out anyway after fifteen minutes.

Bob jumped into the program and ran a report for each employee who wasn’t a Manager, all the way back to when the ERP program was started. Then, he reached out to an employment lawyer and got the okay to refer employees to him.

Bob lined up another job. After he left, every employee in the company got an email with an Excel sheet showing the hours they had put in past forty-four hours a week. The subject line said, “You’re Legally Entitled To Overtime Pay,”  and in the body of the email was the lawyer’s name.

The s*** hit the fan. Almost every employee authorized the lawyer to negotiate with the company on their behalf, and the company had to pay a ton of money.

All the company had to do was pay Bob for the extra work he put in. Instead, they had to pay almost everyone.
